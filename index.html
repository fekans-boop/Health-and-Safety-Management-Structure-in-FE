Restructuring Health and Safety Departments in Further Education

A Framework for Clarity Accountability and Compliance

The Case for Structural Clarity

In Further Education colleges across the UK the health and safety function often operates in ambiguity. Roles blur. Accountability fragments. Competence becomes assumed rather than defined. The consequence is not merely administrative inefficiency but genuine regulatory exposure and compromised duty of care.

This framework proposes a clear structure aligned with ISO 45001 occupational health and safety management systems and the IOSH Competency Framework ensuring that every role carries defined authority measurable competence and transparent accountability.

Proposed Departmental Structure

Head of Health and Safety

Strategic Leadership

The Competent Person under Regulation 7 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999

Core Accountabilities

  • Strategic ownership of the occupational health and safety management system in accordance with ISO 45001 Clauses 5 and 6
  • Direct reporting line to Executive Leadership Team and Corporation Board on health and safety performance context and compliance status
  • Final sign-off authority on all high-risk assessments policy frameworks and governance documentation
  • Leadership of Health and Safety Committee including preparation of board-level reports and strategic risk profiling
  • Oversight of statutory compliance across all college sites activities and third-party arrangements
  • Management of relationships with HSE enforcing authorities insurers and external auditors

Required Competencies

  • Chartered status with IOSH or equivalent professional registration
  • NEBOSH Diploma or equivalent Level 6 qualification minimum
  • Demonstrable experience in educational sector health and safety leadership
  • Evidence of strategic influence at senior leadership level
ISO 45001 Alignment: Clause 5.1 Leadership and commitment, Clause 5.3 Organisational roles responsibilities and authorities
IOSH Framework: Operates at Strategic Level requiring high-level competence in organisational influence and policy development

Health and Safety Manager

Operational Management

Tactical implementation and day-to-day management of health and safety systems

Core Accountabilities

  • Implementation and maintenance of health and safety management systems across all college operations
  • Coordination of risk assessment programmes including general fire manual handling DSE COSHH and site-specific assessments
  • Management of incident investigation processes and implementation of corrective actions in line with ISO 45001 Clause 10
  • Design and delivery of health and safety training programmes for staff and students
  • Coordination of internal audit programmes and preparation for external inspections
  • Oversight of contractor management and external site visit safety protocols

Required Competencies

  • NEBOSH Certificate or equivalent Level 3 qualification minimum
  • Graduate membership of IOSH or working towards chartered status
  • Practical experience in educational or complex multi-site environments
  • Evidence of effective training delivery and stakeholder engagement
ISO 45001 Alignment: Clause 8 Operation including 8.1 Operational planning and control, Clause 9 Performance evaluation
IOSH Framework: Operates at Tactical Level requiring competence in system implementation and technical application

Health and Safety Officer

Operational Support

Frontline delivery of health and safety activities and departmental administration

Core Accountabilities

  • Conduct of routine workplace inspections and safety tours across college sites
  • Support for risk assessment processes including data gathering site visits and documentation
  • Administration of health and safety records including training matrices incident logs and compliance documentation
  • First point of contact for staff queries on health and safety matters
  • Coordination of practical arrangements for drills inspections and safety events
  • Assistance with preparation of committee papers reports and statistical analysis

Required Competencies

  • IOSH Managing Safely or equivalent foundation qualification
  • Working towards NEBOSH Certificate or higher qualification
  • Strong administrative and organisational skills
  • Ability to communicate health and safety requirements to diverse audiences
ISO 45001 Alignment: Clause 7.5 Documented information, Clause 9.1.1 Monitoring measurement analysis and performance evaluation
IOSH Framework: Operates at Operational Level requiring competence in practical application and compliance monitoring

Fire Safety Coordinator

Specialist Function

Dedicated oversight of fire safety compliance and emergency preparedness

Core Accountabilities

  • Ownership of fire risk assessment programme across all college premises in accordance with the Regulatory Reform Fire Safety Order 2005
  • Coordination of fire warden networks emergency evacuation procedures and fire drill programmes
  • Liaison with Fire and Rescue Service and management of enforcement notices or advisory correspondence
  • Oversight of fire safety equipment maintenance including detection systems emergency lighting and firefighting equipment
  • Delivery of fire safety training for staff students and visitors
  • Management of Personal Emergency Evacuation Plans for individuals requiring assistance during evacuation

Required Competencies

  • NEBOSH Fire Certificate or Level 3 Fire Safety qualification
  • Fire Risk Assessment competence certified through accredited training
  • Understanding of building regulations means of escape and fire safety engineering principles
  • Experience in multi-occupancy or educational environments
ISO 45001 Alignment: Clause 8.2 Emergency preparedness and response
IOSH Framework: Operates at Technical Specialist Level requiring deep competence in fire safety legislation and technical standards

Principles for Effective Restructuring

  • Separation of Strategic and Operational Functions: The Head of Health and Safety must be positioned to influence policy and governance not consumed by operational tasks that prevent strategic oversight
  • Clear Lines of Accountability: Every function must have a named individual with defined authority to make decisions enforce standards and be held accountable for outcomes
  • Competence as a Foundation: Roles must be matched to demonstrable competence not merely job titles or historical arrangements. Competence must be evidenced through qualification experience and continuing professional development
  • Adequate Resourcing: A single individual cannot discharge the competent person role for a multi-site FE college whilst also performing operational duties. Adequate staffing is a regulatory necessity not a discretionary enhancement
  • Independence from Operational Pressures: The health and safety function must retain sufficient independence from operational management to challenge unsafe practices and escalate concerns without compromise

Reflection: Why Structure Matters

In many FE colleges health and safety exists in a state of structural ambiguity. Roles are described in general terms. Responsibilities are shared to the point of diffusion. Competence is assumed rather than verified. This is not deliberate negligence. It is organisational drift.

But when an incident occurs when an HSE investigation begins when a civil claim is brought the question asked is always the same. Who was accountable? Who had the authority to prevent this? Who possessed the competence to identify the risk?

Structural clarity is not bureaucracy. It is the foundation of duty of care.

ISO 45001 requires organisations to define roles responsibilities and authorities and communicate them. The IOSH Competency Framework requires that individuals performing health and safety roles possess competence commensurate with the complexity risk and scope of their responsibilities. UK legislation requires that employers appoint competent persons to assist in meeting their legal duties.

The question is not whether these requirements exist. The question is whether your organisational structure makes compliance possible or impossible.

When health and safety is positioned as a strategic function with clear authority adequate resource and demonstrable competence it becomes a source of organisational strength. When it is positioned as an administrative afterthought distributed across multiple unclear roles with insufficient authority it becomes a liability.

The choice is institutional.
